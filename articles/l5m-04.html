Interleaving: The Counterintuitive Practice That Accelerates Mastery

Blocked practice feels productive. Interleaving feels frustrating. The research is unambiguous about which one builds deeper, more flexible knowledge.

VP
Vy Phan
EFL Educator · Doctoral Candidate in Education · Vietnam

Photo by Kelly Sikkema on Unsplash · Free to use

One concept. One technique. One thing you can do today. Final episode of the L5M series: interleaving — the study strategy that consistently outperforms blocked practice in long-term tests, while consistently feeling worse during them. Understanding why it works changes how you design every study session.

Imagine you are learning three types of problems in a mathematics course — say, problems involving volume, surface area, and perimeter. Most study guides, most textbooks, and most students use the same approach: complete all the volume problems first, then all the surface area problems, then all the perimeter problems. Chapter by chapter, type by type, topic by topic.

This is called blocked practice. It is the dominant approach to organised study in most educational systems. It is also, according to a substantial body of research, significantly less effective than its alternative — not for building immediate fluency, but for building the kind of knowledge that transfers, that persists, and that works under the conditions of real use.

The alternative is interleaving: mixing different types of problems, topics, or skills within a single study session, rather than completing all of one type before moving to the next.

The counterintuitive finding is not just that interleaving works better. It is that it works better despite — or more precisely, because — it feels harder and produces more errors during practice.

The surface difference is obvious. But the cognitive difference is deeper: in blocked practice, you always know what kind of problem is coming next. The context tells you which approach to apply before you have even read the problem. In interleaved practice, you must first identify what kind of problem you are facing — which is exactly what real-world application of knowledge requires.

The Rohrer and Taylor Studies: What the Evidence Shows

The modern research programme on interleaving in educational contexts begins most clearly with Doug Rohrer and Kelli Taylor's studies in the mid-2000s, conducted with students working on mathematics problems.

Landmark Study

Rohrer and Taylor (2007) assigned students to practice mathematics problems using either blocked or interleaved sequences. During practice, blocked students performed better — consistent with the fluency that massed, same-type practice produces. But on a test administered one week later, the interleaved group dramatically outperformed the blocked group, despite their lower performance during practice. The advantage was not marginal: interleaved students scored roughly twice as high on the delayed test. The finding held across different problem types and was robust to variations in the ratio of practice problems.

Rohrer, D., & Taylor, K. (2007). The shuffling of mathematics problems improves learning. Instructional Science, 35(6), 481–498.

The key detail — that blocked practice outperformed interleaving during practice but interleaved practice dramatically outperformed blocking on a delayed test — recurs consistently in the interleaving literature. It mirrors the pattern we saw with spacing (L5M-02) and retrieval practice (L5M-03): the strategy that feels more productive in the short term is less productive over time, and the strategy that feels worse is building something that lasts.

Key Research

Kornell and Bjork (2008) extended the interleaving finding to a non-mathematical domain — learning to identify the painting styles of different artists. Participants who studied paintings by each artist in blocked sequences (all Monet, then all Cézanne, then all Braque) judged blocked practice as more effective than interleaved practice. Their test results showed the opposite. The interleaved group was significantly better at identifying the artist's style when shown new paintings they had never seen — the transfer test that actually matters for real-world competence.

Kornell, N., & Bjork, R. A. (2008). Learning concepts and categories: Is spacing the "enemy of induction"? Psychological Science, 19(6), 585–592.

Why Interleaving Works: Two Mechanisms

The effectiveness of interleaving is not arbitrary. Two mechanisms explain it, and understanding them changes how you think about what practice is for.

The first mechanism is discrimination learning. When problems of the same type are blocked together, you never have to identify what kind of problem you are facing — the block tells you. When they are interleaved, every problem requires a prior cognitive step: is this a volume problem or a surface area problem? What features of this sentence tell me whether to use the present perfect or the simple past? What kind of logical fallacy is this? This identification step is cognitively demanding. It is also precisely the skill that real-world application of knowledge requires.

The second mechanism connects to what we covered in earlier episodes: retrieval difficulty. Each interleaved problem requires you to retrieve the relevant approach from memory rather than applying it from immediate working memory. The approach is not active from the previous problem — it has been partially displaced by a different problem in between. Retrieving it requires the same kind of effortful memory access that spaced retrieval practice involves. And as we saw in L5M-02 and L5M-03, that effortful retrieval strengthens the memory trace in ways that smooth, uninterrupted practice does not.

Blocked practice optimises the practice session. Interleaved practice optimises what you can do when the practice session is over.

The Vietnamese Classroom and the Grammar Block

The grip of blocked practice on educational culture is particularly visible in EFL grammar instruction — which is, not coincidentally, one of the domains where the interleaving effect has been most clearly demonstrated.

The standard approach to grammar in Vietnamese EFL classrooms mirrors textbook structure almost exactly: present perfect week, passive voice week, conditionals week. Students work problem sets of twenty present-perfect sentences, followed by twenty passive-voice transformations. By the end of the week, they can produce the target structure fluidly. By the end of the month, when they encounter a sentence that might be present perfect or might be simple past, they are frequently lost — not because they have forgotten the rule, but because they have never practised the prior identification step that real language use requires.

The interleaved alternative does not mean abandoning structure. It means that once a structure has been introduced, subsequent practice should mix it with previously learned structures. Not twenty present-perfect sentences, but twenty sentences where the student must determine which tense is appropriate and why. The content is the same. The cognitive demand — and the resulting learning — is qualitatively different.

Key Research

Pan et al. (2019) demonstrated the interleaving advantage in a foreign language vocabulary learning context, finding that interleaved study of word meanings produced stronger long-term retention than blocked study. Critically, the advantage was strongest for transfer to new contexts — exactly the condition that matters for real language use. Students who blocked their vocabulary study performed better on immediate tests. Students who interleaved outperformed on delayed tests and on tasks requiring flexible use of the vocabulary.

Pan, S. C., Tajran, J., Lovelett, J., Osuna, J., & Rickard, T. C. (2019). Does interleaved practice enhance foreign language learning? The effects of training schedule on Spanish verb conjugation skills. Journal of Educational Psychology, 111(7), 1172–1188.

A Caution: When Not to Interleave

Intellectual honesty requires acknowledging the limits of the effect. Interleaving is most powerful once learners have some familiarity with the material being mixed. For genuinely novel content — for a structure or concept encountered for the first time — some degree of blocked initial exposure is necessary before interleaving can be productive.

Think of it this way: you cannot interleave what you do not yet know exists. A student who has never seen the present perfect cannot benefit from encountering it mixed with past simple — because they have not yet built the schema that interleaved practice strengthens. Interleaving works by making retrieval and discrimination harder. For genuinely new material, it makes them impossible.

The practical implication: use blocked practice for initial introduction of new material, then switch to interleaved practice for review and consolidation. The threshold is lower than most students assume — once you understand a concept well enough to attempt problems independently, you are ready to interleave.

Putting It Together: The L5M Toolkit

This is the fourth and final episode of the Learning in 5 Minutes series. Across these four episodes, we have covered the four most evidence-backed learning strategies in cognitive psychology:

From L5M-01: genuine learning requires moving through Exposure, Understanding, and Retention — and most studying stops too early. The Close-the-Book Test is the simplest way to check where you actually are. From L5M-02: the spacing effect — reviewing material at increasing intervals rather than massing all study into one session — produces dramatically better long-term retention at lower total time cost. From L5M-03: retrieval practice — testing yourself rather than re-reading — is the single most powerful study technique available, and its power comes precisely from the difficulty and error it produces. And now, from L5M-04: interleaving — mixing different problem types, topics, or skills in practice — builds the discrimination and flexible retrieval that real-world application of knowledge requires.

These are not four separate techniques. They are four expressions of the same underlying principle: the conditions that make learning feel easy are not the conditions that make learning durable. The techniques that feel productive are, in each case, optimised for the practice session rather than for what you can do six months later. The techniques that feel harder, produce more errors, and seem less efficient are, in each case, building memory structures that survive time and transfer to new contexts.

Interleaved practice — how to start with any subject
Step 1 Identify 2–3 topics, structures, or problem types you have already studied and understand at a basic level.
Step 2 Create or find practice problems from each type. Mix them into a single list in random order — not all of type A, then all of type B.
Step 3 For each problem, identify what type it is before solving it. This identification step is not optional — it is half the learning.
Step 4 Accept the difficulty. Errors during interleaved practice are expected and productive. They signal that the discrimination you need to build is not yet automatic.
For language Mix grammar structures, tenses, or vocabulary sets — never do twenty of the same type in sequence once you have had initial instruction.
Why it works Interleaving forces the identification step that real use requires, and the retrieval demand of switching between types strengthens each memory trace more than blocked repetition can.
Your one action today
Take your next practice set and deliberately mix it up before you start.
Before your next study session, look at what you plan to practise. If it is all one type — all the same grammar structure, all the same kind of maths problem, all vocabulary from one unit — mix in at least one or two items from something you studied earlier. Make the session harder. That difficulty is the point.
If you want to combine the full L5M toolkit: space your next review by 48 hours (L5M-02), start with retrieval rather than re-reading (L5M-03), and mix problem types rather than blocking them (L5M-04). These techniques compound — each one makes the others more effective.
