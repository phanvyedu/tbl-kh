What Happens to English Learning When AI Can Translate Everything?

Krashen's i+1 formula worked when comprehensible input was scarce. Now that AI can remove all friction — what happens to acquisition?

VP
Vy Phan
EFL Educator · Doctoral Candidate in Education · Vietnam

Photo by Aaron Burden on Unsplash · Free to use

Think about the last time you encountered a word in English that you didn't know. What did you do? If you reached for a translation app before even attempting to infer the meaning from context — this article is about that moment, and what it costs you. Not in terms of effort. In terms of whether the word becomes yours.

My students have Google Translate on their phones. They have AI chatbots that will not only translate a sentence but rewrite it in fluent English, explain the grammar, and generate five similar examples. They have, in other words, access to a kind of linguistic scaffolding that no generation of language learners has ever had before.

I have been teaching English as a foreign language in Vietnam for fifteen years. I have watched the relationship between my students and English change profoundly — not because of any curriculum reform, but because of this. The friction of not-understanding, which was once an unavoidable feature of language learning, has become optional.

And that, I want to argue, is a serious problem. Not because the tools are bad. But because the friction they remove is the friction that produces acquisition.

Krashen's Input Hypothesis: What It Actually Says

Stephen Krashen's Input Hypothesis, first formalised in the early 1980s, makes a deceptively simple claim: language acquisition occurs when learners are exposed to input that is comprehensible and slightly beyond their current level of competence. He formalised this as i+1 — where i represents current competence and +1 represents the next level of complexity.

The hypothesis has two critical components that are often separated but should not be. The first is comprehensibility — the input must be understood well enough for meaning to be extracted. The second is challenge — it must contain elements the learner does not yet fully control. Both conditions are necessary. Incomprehensible input produces no acquisition. Fully understood input produces no acquisition either. The engine runs on the gap.

Key Theory

Krashen distinguished between acquisition — the unconscious, implicit process by which we develop language competence through meaningful exposure — and learning — the conscious, explicit study of grammar rules and vocabulary. His controversial but influential argument was that only acquisition produces fluent, spontaneous language use. Learned rules can only be applied consciously, under low-stress conditions, with time to monitor output.

Krashen, S. D. (1982). Principles and practice in second language acquisition. Pergamon Press.

Krashen also articulated the Affective Filter Hypothesis — the idea that anxiety, low motivation, and lack of self-confidence raise a metaphorical filter that blocks input from reaching the language acquisition device, even when that input is comprehensible. The emotional conditions of learning matter as much as the cognitive ones.

This last point will become important when we consider what AI does to the Vietnamese EFL classroom.

The i+1 Problem with Instant Translation

Consider what happens when a student encounters an unknown word in a reading text. In Krashen's model, this encounter — if the surrounding context is comprehensible enough — is precisely where acquisition can occur. The learner uses contextual clues, draws on existing knowledge, makes an inference. Even a failed inference followed by a dictionary check involves more processing than a direct translation.

This is not a sentimental argument for difficulty. It is a claim grounded in what we know about how language is internalised. Michael Long's Interaction Hypothesis extended Krashen's model by arguing that negotiation of meaning — the back-and-forth of trying to be understood and trying to understand — is particularly powerful for acquisition. When a learner pushes through incomprehension, they are not simply accessing a word. They are building the network of associations that makes the word retrievable later.

Key Theory

Long's Interaction Hypothesis (1981, revised 1996) argued that conversational interaction provides learners with modified input, negative feedback, and opportunities to notice gaps in their interlanguage — all of which accelerate acquisition beyond what comprehensible input alone can achieve. The key mechanism is negotiation of meaning: the learner's active attempt to resolve communication breakdown.

Long, M. H. (1996). The role of the linguistic environment in second language acquisition. In W. C. Ritchie & T. K. Bhatia (Eds.), Handbook of second language acquisition (pp. 413–468). Academic Press.

Instant translation short-circuits this process entirely. The unknown word is rendered into Vietnamese before the learner has had time to attempt inference, before any negotiation of meaning has occurred, before the word has had the chance to become i+1. What arrives is not a foreign word that the learner has partially processed. It is a Vietnamese word wearing English clothes.

The friction of not-understanding is not a design flaw in language learning. It is the mechanism itself.

What AI Tutors Get Right — and What They Miss

I want to be precise here, because the argument is not that AI language tools are uniformly harmful. They are not. And dismissing them entirely would be both intellectually dishonest and pedagogically impractical.

AI tutors can, in principle, calibrate input to a learner's current level — potentially better than a human teacher managing thirty students simultaneously. They can provide immediate feedback on pronunciation, grammar, and vocabulary that a classroom teacher cannot scale. They can offer patient, low-stakes practice that reduces the affective filter, particularly for anxious learners who would rather fail privately than publicly.

Function AI tutor Krashen alignment
Input calibration Can adjust complexity to approximate i+1 Strong — if calibration is accurate
Affective filter Low-stakes, patient, non-judgemental Strong — reduces anxiety barrier
Instant translation Removes all lexical friction immediately Weak — eliminates the productive gap
Output production Generates polished output for the learner Weak — bypasses the learner's own system
Meaning negotiation Simulates interaction but cannot replicate genuine communication breakdown Partial — lacks authentic communicative stakes

The fundamental problem is not any single feature. It is the default mode of use. When AI is primarily used to remove difficulty — to translate, to rewrite, to correct — it produces the same cognitive pattern we identified in the previous episode: the learner receives a polished output that bypassed their own developing system. The language was processed by the AI. Not by the learner.

A Classroom Story from Vietnam

I want to describe something I observed — not as data, but as a phenomenon worth sitting with.

I assigned my students a short reading task: a news article at approximately B1 level, with several unfamiliar words. I asked them to read it, infer meaning from context, and write a brief summary in their own words. One student submitted a summary that was notably more fluent than anything she had written before. When I asked her to discuss the article in class, she struggled to recall basic details — the topic, the main argument, the examples used.

She had understood the article, in the sense that she had produced a competent summary. But she had not processed it. The AI had. Her summary was accurate. Her comprehension was shallow.

The gap between producing correct output and genuine acquisition plays out in classrooms every day. Photo: Unsplash

This is Krashen's distinction between acquisition and learning made visible in real time. She had learned, in a narrow sense, what the article was about. But she had not acquired anything — no new vocabulary had entered her developing interlanguage, no new grammatical structure had been processed, no encounter with authentic English at i+1 had taken place.

The AI had completed the comprehension task. She had completed the submission task. These are not the same thing.

The Vietnamese Context: Confucian Inheritance and the Affective Filter

Krashen's Affective Filter Hypothesis takes on particular resonance in the Vietnamese EFL context, and I think this is underappreciated in discussions of AI and language learning.

The Vietnamese classroom carries a Confucian educational inheritance — one that values precision over approximation, correct answers over productive risk-taking, and deference to authority over exploratory learning. In such a context, making mistakes in English in front of peers and teachers is not merely uncomfortable. For many students, it is associated with genuine shame.

AI lowers the affective filter dramatically. A student who would never attempt to speak English spontaneously in class will happily practice with an AI chatbot for an hour. This is not trivial. If AI can reduce language anxiety sufficiently to increase the volume of practice, that is a genuine benefit — even if the quality of that practice is imperfect.

The question is not whether AI lowers the affective filter. It clearly does. The question is what happens to acquisition once the filter is lowered — if AI is simultaneously removing the productive challenge that acquisition requires.

Lowering the filter and removing the input gap is not the same as acquisition-friendly use of AI. It may produce more comfortable learners without producing more proficient ones.

Toward a Principled Use of AI in EFL Classrooms

The framework Krashen gives us suggests specific principles for AI use in language learning — principles that are not about restricting AI but about understanding where it helps acquisition and where it substitutes for it.

Use AI to adjust input level, not to remove all difficulty. An AI that presents a text at the right level of challenge, with contextual support but without instant translation, is aligned with the i+1 principle. An AI that translates every unknown word on demand is not.

Use AI to increase practice volume under low-stakes conditions. If AI tutors allow anxious learners to produce more English — more speaking, more writing, more interaction — that is acquisition-aligned, provided the learner is doing the producing.

Preserve the learner's own output. The moment AI begins generating language on the learner's behalf — translating their writing into better English, correcting their errors before they have had time to notice them — the acquisition loop is broken. The learner needs to produce, notice, adjust. AI can inform that cycle. It should not replace it.

Design tasks where AI assistance is structurally impossible. Oral interaction, in-class discussion, spontaneous speaking tasks — these remain the spaces where the learner must operate their own developing system. They are not technological conservatives. They are acquisition environments.

None of this requires abandoning AI. It requires thinking carefully about which aspects of language learning depend on the productive struggle that AI currently tends to eliminate.

Keep learning. Keep becoming.
