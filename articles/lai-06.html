AI Policy in Vietnamese Education: What the Guidelines Say and What They Miss

Vietnam has some of the most ambitious AI education policies in Southeast Asia. Ambition and implementation are different things — and the gap between them is where teachers and students actually live.

VP
Vy Phan
EFL Educator · Doctoral Candidate in Education · Vietnam

Photo by Campaign Creators on Unsplash · Free to use

Before you read: think about the last time a policy decision — from a government, an institution, a school — arrived in your classroom. How long did it take to get there? What changed in translation? What was lost? This article is about the distance between where AI policy is written and where learning actually happens — and why that distance matters more than the policy itself.

Vietnam is, by several measures, one of the most AI-ready nations in Southeast Asia. In the World AI Index 2025, it ranked sixth globally — ahead of many developed countries — scoring particularly high on trust in AI and acceptance of AI applications. A 2025 survey found that 64% of Vietnamese teachers had used AI in their teaching activities, ranking fifth among participating countries and significantly above the OECD average of 36%.

The policy landscape is equally ambitious. In December 2024, the Politburo issued Resolution 57-NQ/TW, setting the goal that by 2030, Vietnam will be among the top three countries in Southeast Asia in AI research and development. The Ministry of Education and Training has been moving rapidly: a pilot curriculum framework for AI education in general schools was unveiled in early 2026, with plans to review, refine and scale up across the system.

By any measure, this is serious policy ambition. Vietnam is not a country that is watching AI from a distance and wondering whether to engage. It has decided to lead.

And yet — sitting in a classroom in Đồng Nai, reading these announcements alongside the five questions that have driven this series — I find myself holding two things simultaneously. Genuine admiration for the ambition. And a set of questions that the policy documents do not answer.

This final episode of the LAI series is about those questions.

What the Policy Framework Actually Contains

Before critiquing policy, it is worth understanding what it actually says. The Vietnamese AI-in-education policy landscape has several distinct layers, each operating at different levels of specificity and authority.

Key policy documents — Vietnam AI in education, 2024–2026
Resolution 57-NQ/TW
Dec 2024, Politburo
Establishes AI as a national strategic priority. Sets the target of top-3 Southeast Asia in AI R&D by 2030. Allocates at least 3% of national budget to science, technology, and digital transformation.
MoET Draft Curriculum Framework

Proposes AI as a formal subject across all levels of general schooling. Framework divided into four strands: human-centred thinking, ethics, technical applications, and system design.
National AI Strategy for Education 2035
2025, MoET + UNICEF
Developed with UNICEF support. Includes digital competency frameworks for teachers and students, capacity-building roadmap, and pilots of AI-powered tools in classrooms.
AI Law (Digital Technology Industry Law)
Effective March 2026
First legal framework for AI in Vietnam. Defines principles for developing, providing, and deploying AI. Establishes sandbox mechanisms and ethical accountability standards.
Pilot Program
Dec 2025 – May 2026
AI education piloted in selected schools nationwide. Review in June 2026, followed by broader rollout from 2026–2027 academic year.

Reading this framework as a whole, several things stand out. The ambition is genuine and the timeline is fast. The ethical dimension is present — the curriculum framework explicitly includes human-centred thinking and the AI Law addresses accountability. A survey of more than 11,000 students found that 87% were aware of AI applications in education, but major challenges included limited knowledge and skills, inadequate equipment and technology, and insufficient teacher guidance.

That last finding — "insufficient teacher guidance" — is where the policy framework and classroom reality begin to diverge.

The Implementation Gap: What Policy Cannot See

Implementation gap theory — the study of why policy intentions fail to translate into practice — has a long history in educational research. The core finding is consistent across contexts: policies designed at the system level routinely underestimate the complexity of the institutional, cultural, and relational conditions that determine whether they work.

Key Research

Fullan's framework for educational change argues that successful implementation requires attention to three dimensions simultaneously: the use of new materials and technologies, changes in teaching practice and behaviour, and changes in beliefs and understandings. Most policy failures focus on the first dimension while neglecting the second and third. In the context of AI in education, this means: providing teachers with AI tools (dimension 1) without changing how they teach (dimension 2) or how they think about learning (dimension 3) is unlikely to produce meaningful educational change.

Fullan, M. (2007). The new meaning of educational change (4th ed.). Teachers College Press.

In Vietnam's case, the implementation challenges are not primarily technical. The infrastructure is improving; the political will is evident; the tools are becoming available. The challenges are pedagogical and psychological — and they are precisely the ones that the policy documents address least.

Consider what the MoET survey of nearly 35,000 teachers in early 2025 found: 76% had used AI in classrooms. On the surface, this is remarkable. But using AI and using AI well — in ways that support genuine learning rather than simply automating existing practice — are different things. A teacher who uses AI to generate worksheets has "used AI in the classroom." A teacher who uses AI to give students individualised feedback on drafts, to prompt Socratic questioning, or to surface misconceptions in real time is doing something categorically different. The policy framework does not distinguish between these uses.

The question is not whether Vietnamese teachers are using AI. They are. The question is what kind of learning that AI use is producing — and whether anyone is asking that question at the policy level.

The Competency Problem: Training Versus Understanding

The Vietnamese policy framework places heavy emphasis on teacher training and competency development. UNICEF has supported the development of national frameworks on Digital and AI competencies and capacity-building initiatives for both teachers and students. STEAM for Vietnam has aimed to bring AI training to every teacher across the country.

This is necessary work. But training in AI tools and developing genuine understanding of AI's implications for learning are different things — and the policy framework tends to conflate them.

Teacher training programmes in AI typically focus on tool use: how to operate specific platforms, how to integrate AI into lesson planning, how to use AI for assessment. These are real competencies. But they do not address the deeper questions that this series has explored: What happens to learning when AI removes productive struggle? How does Confucian face-concern interact with AI feedback systems? What motivational conditions determine whether AI adoption serves learning or avoids it?

These are not questions that can be answered in a training session. They require the kind of sustained, theoretically-informed reflection that doctoral programmes and research communities produce — but which rarely reaches the classroom teacher in a form that is practically useful.

Key Research

Darling-Hammond et al.'s synthesis of teacher professional development research identifies five features of effective PD: it is content-focused; incorporates active learning; supports collaboration; uses models of effective practice; and provides coaching and expert support. Crucially, it is sustained and intensive — not one-off training sessions. The evidence consistently shows that training that does not change practice is training that has not worked, regardless of how many teachers it reaches.

Darling-Hammond, L., Hyler, M. E., & Gardner, M. (2017). Effective teacher professional development. Learning Policy Institute.

The ambition to train every Vietnamese teacher in AI is admirable. The risk is that it produces widespread shallow competency — teachers who can use the tools but have not been supported to think critically about what the tools do to learning. This is not a failure of the teachers. It is a failure of the policy framework to distinguish between training and understanding.

The Equity Problem the Policy Does Not Name

Vietnam is a country of significant geographic, economic, and infrastructural inequality. Nearly 120,000 teacher positions remain unfilled as of 2025, concentrated particularly in preschool and general education. The digital infrastructure that AI tools require is unevenly distributed across urban and rural contexts, between provinces with strong economic bases and those without.

The policy framework acknowledges this — it speaks of "broad access" and "equitable rollout." But the equity problem in AI education goes deeper than infrastructure. It goes to the question of who benefits from AI tools even when they are equally available.

As I argued in LAI-03 and LAI-04, AI tutors embed assumptions about learners that align more closely with certain cultural and psychological profiles than others. The student with high self-efficacy and low face-concern will use AI tools in ways that accelerate learning. The student shaped by performance-avoidance goals and a fragile sense of competence will use AI to bypass learning or avoid it entirely.

These are not individual failures. They are systematic effects — and they will tend to reproduce existing educational inequalities rather than reduce them. A policy framework that deploys AI tools widely without attending to the motivational and cultural conditions that determine how those tools are used will, by default, advantage already-advantaged learners.

The Ethics Gap: From Principles to Practice

The Vietnamese AI policy framework is notable for including ethics explicitly. The new curriculum framework incorporates ethical considerations as one of its four strands. The AI Law establishes accountability principles. These are genuine advances.

But ethical principles at the policy level and ethical guidance at the classroom level are different things — and the distance between them is rarely acknowledged.

A curriculum framework that teaches students to "recognise algorithmic bias" is engaging with a real and important issue. But the student who needs to understand algorithmic bias most urgently is not the student in an AI ethics class. It is the student whose university application is being evaluated by an algorithm that was trained on data that does not represent them. It is the student who receives AI-generated feedback calibrated to a learner profile that does not match their cultural context. It is the student whose silence — culturally meaningful, as I argued in LAI-03 — is interpreted by an AI system as disengagement.

These are not abstract ethics questions. They are immediate, practical ones that appear in classrooms every day. And they require not just principles but the kind of practitioner knowledge and critical literacy that sustained engagement with learning science and cultural context produces.

The gap between "AI must be ethical" and "here is how to handle this specific situation with this specific student" is the gap where most teachers currently live — without adequate support.

What Good Policy Would Look Like

I want to be clear: I am not arguing that Vietnam's AI education policy is bad. By regional and global standards, it is genuinely impressive — in its ambition, its pace, its inclusion of ethical considerations, and its commitment to equity in principle. The problem is not the direction. It is the depth.

Research on effective educational technology policy — synthesised by Selwyn and others — identifies several conditions that distinguish policies that improve learning from policies that improve adoption statistics.

Key Research

Selwyn's critical analysis of educational technology policy argues that effective policy requires a shift from "technology as solution" to "technology as context" — from asking "how do we deploy AI?" to asking "what learning are we trying to produce, and under what conditions does AI help or hinder that?" This reframing is not anti-technology. It is a more demanding standard for technology, one that requires policy to engage seriously with learning science rather than treating pedagogy as a secondary consideration.

Selwyn, N. (2011). Education and technology: Key issues and debates. Continuum International Publishing.

What would this look like in the Vietnamese context? At minimum, it would require: professional development that goes beyond tool training to include sustained engagement with learning science; assessment frameworks that evaluate not just whether students use AI but whether their learning deepens; pedagogical guidance that attends to the specific cultural conditions of Vietnamese classrooms; and equity analysis that asks not just whether AI tools are available but whether all students can use them in ways that support rather than undermine learning.

None of this is technically difficult to design. It requires political will, time, and the willingness to treat pedagogy as seriously as technology — which is, ultimately, a question of what education is understood to be for.

A Note from the Classroom

I write this as a teacher in Đồng Nai, not as a policy analyst in Hanoi. The distance between those two positions is part of what I have been trying to articulate across this series.

The policy documents that shape how AI enters Vietnamese classrooms are written by people who care about education. I do not doubt their commitment. But they are written at a level of abstraction that makes the specific texture of a specific classroom — its silences, its face dynamics, its particular students and their particular fears — invisible. Not because policymakers are indifferent to classrooms, but because policy, by its nature, operates at a scale that cannot see individual learners.

This series has tried to do something that policy cannot: to take those individual learners seriously, to bring learning science into contact with Vietnamese classroom reality, and to ask what AI actually does — not in principle, but in the specific cognitive and cultural conditions where Vietnamese teachers and students meet.

The policy will continue to develop. The tools will continue to improve. The questions will remain the same: What is learning for? Who is it serving? And are we building the conditions — pedagogical, psychological, cultural — in which the technology can actually help?

Keep learning. Keep becoming.
— Vy
