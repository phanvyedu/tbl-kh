Who Is the Teacher When AI Is in the Room?

If AI can explain, scaffold, give feedback, and adapt — the question is no longer what teachers do. It is what teaching is for.

VP
Vy Phan
EFL Educator · Doctoral Candidate in Education · Vietnam

Photo by NeONBRAND on Unsplash · Free to use

Before you read: think about the teacher who most shaped how you think — not just what you know. What did they do that an AI could not? Hold that answer loosely as you read. This article will try to put pressure on it — not to dismiss it, but to understand it more precisely.

I have been a teacher for fifteen years. The question I find myself sitting with now — and cannot easily leave — is this: what was I actually doing all that time?

I was explaining things. AI can explain things. I was giving feedback. AI can give feedback. I was scaffolding difficult tasks, adjusting to individual needs, asking questions designed to provoke thinking. AI can do versions of all of these. Not perfectly, not always, not without significant limitations — but well enough that the question becomes uncomfortable.

If AI can perform the identifiable functions of teaching, then what is left? Is there something in teaching that is not reducible to its functions — something that AI cannot, even in principle, do? Or is the discomfort I feel simply the discomfort of a professional group watching their functional monopoly erode, as other professional groups have before them?

I do not think the honest answer is obvious. And I am suspicious of responses that arrive too quickly — both the defensive claim that teachers are irreplaceable and the enthusiastic claim that AI will transform education by replacing them. Both reach conclusions before the question has been taken seriously.

This episode is an attempt to take the question seriously.

What Teachers Actually Do: A Functional Inventory

Before asking what AI changes, we need clarity about what teaching involves. The literature on teacher functions is extensive, but for our purposes, a useful framework comes from distinguishing between instructional, relational, and developmental functions.

Teacher functions — what AI can and cannot currently do
Content explanation
AI capable
Presenting information clearly, adjusting complexity, offering multiple representations of a concept, answering content questions at any hour.
Scaffolded feedback
AI capable
Identifying errors, suggesting corrections, guiding revision, calibrating task difficulty to learner level in real time.
Motivation and affect regulation
Partial — contested
Maintaining engagement, managing anxiety, restoring confidence after failure. AI can simulate warmth; whether it can provide genuine psychological safety is unresolved.
Moral and relational modelling
Human essential
Demonstrating intellectual honesty, modelling productive struggle, showing what it looks like to care about ideas and people simultaneously. Cannot be simulated without loss.
Curriculum judgement
Human essential
Deciding what is worth learning and in what order — questions that require values, not just optimization. AI can execute a curriculum; it cannot adjudicate between competing visions of what education is for.
Reading the room
Human essential
Interpreting collective atmosphere, sensing unspoken dynamics, responding to what is not said. Requires embodied presence and cultural fluency that AI currently cannot access.
Bearing witness
Human essential
Being genuinely present to a student's struggle and growth in a way that makes that struggle and growth matter. A fundamentally relational act that requires a subject who cares.

The pattern in this table is not random. AI performs the functions that are most easily decomposed into information processing: presenting, evaluating, adjusting. It struggles with — or cannot perform — the functions that are relational in a deeper sense: moral modelling, collective reading, genuine witnessing.

This distinction maps onto a fundamental divide in philosophy of education between teaching as instruction and teaching as formation.

Instruction Versus Formation: What Education Is Actually For

The distinction between instruction and formation goes back to the ancient Greek division between didaskalia (teaching as imparting knowledge) and paideia (education as the cultivation of a fully human person). In modern educational theory, it surfaces in debates between cognitive approaches to learning and humanistic or relational approaches.

Instruction, narrowly conceived, is the transfer of knowledge and skill from a more competent agent to a less competent one. If this is what teaching is, then AI is already a better teacher than most humans in many domains — faster, more patient, more consistent, available at any hour, infinitely scalable.

Formation is something different. It is the process by which a person comes to inhabit knowledge — to have it become part of how they see the world, make decisions, and relate to others. It is what the German educational tradition called Bildung: the cultivation not merely of competence but of the whole person.

Key Concept

The concept of Bildung, developed through the work of Humboldt, Hegel, and later Gadamer, describes education as a process of self-formation through engagement with culture, ideas, and other people. It is irreducibly dialogical — it cannot happen in isolation, and it cannot happen through mere exposure to information. It requires the kind of encounter with genuine otherness that transforms the self. Whether AI can provide genuine otherness — whether it can be an other in the philosophically necessary sense — is a question that current AI systems cannot answer.

Gadamer, H.-G. (1975). Truth and method. Seabury Press. (Original work published 1960)

The teacher's role in formation is not primarily informational. It is testimonial. A teacher who loves mathematics, who is visibly moved by a proof's elegance, who struggles in front of students with a problem they have not solved before — this teacher is not primarily transmitting content. They are testifying to the value of intellectual engagement. They are showing, in their person, what it looks like to take ideas seriously.

AI cannot testify. It can produce testimony-shaped text. But testimony requires a subject who actually cares — and caring, in the philosophically relevant sense, requires consciousness, vulnerability, and stakes. An AI tutor that says "that's a great insight!" is not a witness to the student's thinking. It is a pattern-matching system producing a socially expected response.

The Vygotskian Problem: What Kind of Other Does Learning Require?

Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development — the space between what a learner can do alone and what they can do with guidance — is often cited in discussions of AI tutoring as evidence that AI can play the scaffolding role. The AI calibrates to the learner's current level, provides support that enables performance just beyond independent capacity, and gradually withdraws as competence develops. This is a plausible account of AI-assisted instruction.

But Vygotsky's framework contains a dimension that this account misses. For Vygotsky, the zone of proximal development is not merely a cognitive gap to be bridged by any available scaffold. It is a social space — one that is constituted by the relationship between the learner and a more knowledgeable other who is genuinely invested in the learner's development.

Key Theory

Vygotsky's concept of the ZPD is inseparable from his broader social constructivist framework, in which all higher psychological functions originate in social interaction before becoming internalised. The "more knowledgeable other" in Vygotsky's model is not simply a more competent performer — they are a social being whose relationship with the learner mediates the learning process. The quality of that relationship — its emotional texture, its history, its mutuality — affects what can be learned and how deeply.

Vygotsky, L. S. (1978). Mind in society: The development of higher psychological processes. Harvard University Press.

The question AI poses to Vygotsky's framework is not whether it can scaffold — it clearly can. The question is whether a non-social entity can constitute a genuine "more knowledgeable other" in the sense the framework requires. Can the ZPD be opened by a system that has no investment in the learner's development — no stake in whether they flourish?

I think the honest answer is: partially, and under certain conditions. AI can scaffold cognitive operations effectively. What it cannot do is care whether the learner grows as a person — and that caring, I want to argue, is not a peripheral feature of the teacher's role. It is what transforms instruction into formation.

Noddings, Care Ethics, and What the Classroom Relationship Provides

Nel Noddings' ethics of care offers perhaps the most precise account of what human teaching provides that AI cannot. For Noddings, genuine caring — in the educational context — requires three elements: engrossment (the carer genuinely attends to the cared-for's reality), motivational displacement (the carer's energy is directed by the cared-for's needs rather than their own agenda), and confirmation (the carer sees and affirms the best possible version of who the cared-for is becoming).

Key Theory

Noddings argued that the caring relation — not the delivery of content — is the foundation of genuine education. A teacher who cares, in Noddings' sense, perceives the student's reality as their own, asks what this particular student needs (not what students in general need), and holds a vision of who this student could become that the student themselves may not yet hold. This is irreducibly personal — it requires knowing the individual, not the category.

Noddings, N. (1984). Caring: A feminine approach to ethics and moral education. University of California Press.

Can an AI tutor be engrossed in a student's reality? It can model engrossment — produce responses that appear to attend carefully. But engrossment, in Noddings' sense, requires that the carer's own reality be temporarily set aside — requires a self that can be set aside. An AI system has no self to set aside. It has no competing preoccupations, no fatigue, no personal history with this student that colours the interaction. The very consistency that makes AI tutors attractive as instructional tools is evidence of their incapacity for genuine caring.

Confirmation — affirming the best possible version of who the student is becoming — requires a vision of human flourishing that goes beyond performance on tasks. It requires the teacher to hold a conception of the good life, of what a fully realised version of this particular student might look like, and to act from that conception. This is a moral act, not an informational one. It requires the teacher to have a stake in the student's humanity.

AI can tell a student what they got right. It cannot believe in who they might become.

Educator Identity Under Pressure: The Vietnamese Context

In the Vietnamese educational context, the question of teacher identity carries particular weight. The Confucian framing of the teacher — as moral exemplar, knowledge custodian, and relationship anchor — invests the role with significance that extends well beyond instruction. The Vietnamese term thầy (teacher, but also master, guide) carries relational and moral connotations that no translation quite captures.

When AI enters this context, the threat to teacher identity is not primarily about job displacement — though that anxiety exists. It is about meaning. If the most measurable things a teacher does can be done better by a machine, then what remains? And is what remains enough to sustain a professional identity that has been built on being needed?

The relational dimension of teaching — what it means to be genuinely present to a student's struggle — is what AI cannot replicate. Photo: Unsplash

Research on teacher professional identity suggests that it is built around four pillars: competence (I know things my students need to know), authority (I have the right to direct learning), relationship (I matter to my students), and purpose (what I do is important). AI directly challenges the first two. The third and fourth — relationship and purpose — are where teacher identity can find solid ground in an AI age.

Identity pillar AI's challenge What remains
Competence AI knows more than most teachers in most domains, is never wrong in the same ways, and is available 24/7 Pedagogical judgement — knowing how to teach, not just what is known
Authority AI answers questions without hierarchy; students may trust it more than the teacher for factual queries Moral authority — the right to make claims about what matters and why
Relationship AI simulates relational warmth; some students may prefer its consistency and non-judgment Genuine caring — the teacher as a person who is actually invested in this student's growth
Purpose If instruction is automated, the purpose of teaching becomes less obvious Formation — the cultivation of persons, not just competencies

The teacher who grounds their identity in competence and authority — in being the most knowledgeable person in the room — is the teacher whose identity AI most threatens. The teacher who grounds their identity in relationship and purpose is the teacher who has something AI cannot replace. This is not comfortable news, because it requires a shift in how teaching is conceived, valued, and rewarded — a shift that most educational systems have not begun to make.

What This Means for How We Train and Support Teachers

The implications of this analysis are not primarily for individual teachers. They are for the systems that prepare, support, and evaluate them.

Teacher education that focuses primarily on content knowledge and instructional technique is preparing teachers for the functions that AI performs. It is not preparing them for the functions that AI cannot perform — for the relational, moral, and formative work that is the irreducible core of teaching as human practice.

This requires rethinking what teacher preparation is for. Not less emphasis on content — teachers need to know their subjects deeply enough to model genuine intellectual engagement. But alongside content knowledge: explicit preparation for the relational and moral dimensions of teaching. How to bear witness to a student's struggle. How to hold a vision of a student's potential when the student cannot. How to be a person in front of learners — genuinely present, genuinely invested, genuinely uncertain in ways that are productive rather than undermining.

These are not soft skills. They are the hardest skills in teaching. And they are the ones that matter most in a world where AI has taken over the rest.

The teacher AI cannot replace is not the teacher who knows the most. It is the teacher who cares the most — and knows how to make that caring pedagogically useful.

I have been a teacher for fifteen years. I am still learning what that means. But I am increasingly certain that the answer is not in my knowledge of grammar rules or my ability to explain the passive voice. It is in the fifteen years of particular relationships — of students whose names I still remember, whose struggles I still think about, whose growth I had the privilege of witnessing.

AI was not in those relationships. It could not have been. And understanding precisely why it could not is, I think, the most important thing we can do as educators in the age of AI.

Keep learning. Keep becoming.
— Vy
