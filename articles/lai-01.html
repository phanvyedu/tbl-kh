Does AI Make Us Better Learners — or Just Faster Forgetters?

When AI handles the hard cognitive work for us, something important may be getting lost.

VP
Vy Phan
EFL Educator · Doctoral Candidate in Education · Vietnam

Photo by Levart Photographer on Unsplash · Free to use

Before you read: think of the last time you used AI to help you understand something — a concept, a text, a problem. Did you close the tab feeling like you had learned it? Now, a week later, can you explain that thing without the AI? What you can recall freely is what you actually learned. The rest was something else. That gap is what this article is about.

My students in Đồng Nai are using AI. I know this not because they told me — they didn't — but because I recognise the outputs. A paragraph that flows too smoothly. An explanation that uses vocabulary the student has never used in conversation. A homework answer that is technically correct but somehow hollow, like a shell with nothing inside.

I am not here to condemn them. I am doing the same thing. As a doctoral researcher studying learning science, I have used AI to summarise papers, generate outlines, and explain statistical concepts I was struggling with. It is useful. It is fast. And it raises a question I cannot stop thinking about:

When AI does the cognitive heavy lifting for us, are we learning — or are we simply processing?

The answer, I think, lies in a distinction that learning science has understood for decades. A distinction that AI — however sophisticated — does not automatically respect.

The Generation Effect: Why Struggle Is the Point

In 1978, Norman Slamecka and Peter Graf published a study that would quietly reshape how researchers think about memory. They asked participants to learn word pairs — either by reading them (e.g., "fast — SLOW") or by generating the second word themselves from a cue (e.g., "fast — SL__"). The result was striking: participants who generated the words themselves remembered them significantly better than those who simply read them.

This is known as the generation effect — the finding that information we produce ourselves is retained more durably than information we passively receive.

Key Research

Slamecka & Graf (1978) demonstrated that self-generated responses produced reliably stronger memory than read responses, even when the read responses were studied longer. The effect has been replicated across dozens of studies and holds across age groups, content types, and learning contexts.

Slamecka, N. J., & Graf, P. (1978). The generation effect: Delineation of a phenomenon. Journal of Experimental Psychology: Human Learning and Memory, 4(6), 592–604.

Now consider what happens when a student asks AI to explain a concept. The AI generates. The student receives. The cognitive act of producing — of reaching into existing knowledge, making connections, constructing an answer — is bypassed entirely.

The student has been given the answer without doing the work that makes answers stick.

Cognitive Load Theory and the Shortcut Problem

John Sweller's cognitive load theory, developed through the 1980s and refined since, offers a complementary lens. Sweller argued that learning happens when working memory is appropriately challenged — not overwhelmed, but not underutilised either. The goal is to direct cognitive effort toward the schema construction that produces lasting understanding.

The problem with many AI-assisted workflows is not that they reduce cognitive load — that can be useful — but that they reduce the right kind of cognitive load. When a student uses AI to generate a summary of a chapter they haven't read carefully, they skip the effortful processing that would have built the schema in the first place.

Effortful processing — the kind of struggle that cognitive load theory tells us actually builds schemas. Photo: Unsplash

Sweller distinguished between intrinsic load (the inherent complexity of the material), extraneous load (unnecessary cognitive effort from poor design), and germane load (effort that directly contributes to schema formation). Good teaching reduces extraneous load while preserving — even amplifying — germane load.

AI, used thoughtlessly, risks eliminating germane load entirely. It is the most sophisticated form of extraneous support ever invented, and it carries a serious risk: making learning feel easy while leaving nothing behind.

Key Research

Sweller's original cognitive load framework established that working memory is limited and that learning requires managing this limitation intelligently. The key insight for AI-assisted learning: reducing cognitive effort is not always beneficial — the effort required for schema construction is educationally necessary, not merely difficult.

Sweller, J. (1988). Cognitive load during problem solving: Effects on learning. Cognitive Science, 12(2), 257–285.

What I Observed in Đồng Nai

I want to be careful here. I am not making a universal claim. I am describing a pattern I have observed in my own classroom, in a specific cultural and educational context — and inviting you to consider what it might mean in yours.

In the EFL classroom, the production of language is itself the learning. When a student struggles to find the right word, reaches for a grammatical structure they half-remember, makes a mistake and notices it — that is language acquisition happening. Stephen Krashen's Input Hypothesis argued that comprehensible input at i+1 — just beyond current competence — is the engine of acquisition. But input without output, without the student's own production, is only half the equation.

What I see with AI-assisted writing is different. The student produces a text, but the language in that text was never processed through their own developing system. They did not search for the word. They did not make a grammatical decision. They received a polished product that bypassed the productive struggle that would have made the language theirs.

The AI wrote in English. The student, in any meaningful sense, did not.

This is not a Vietnamese problem or an EFL problem. It is a learning science problem. And it applies equally to a graduate student using AI to generate literature review sections, a professional using AI to draft analysis they should be constructing themselves, or a researcher using AI to summarise papers they should be reading.

The Retrieval Gap: What Testing Yourself Reveals

Roediger and Karpicke's 2006 study on retrieval practice showed something that should unsettle every AI-dependent learner. Students who re-studied material performed better on immediate tests. Students who tested themselves — even when they failed to retrieve the answers — performed dramatically better one week later.

Condition Immediate test One week later
Re-study (read repeatedly) Strong Weak — significant forgetting
Retrieval practice (self-test) Moderate Strong — durable retention

The implication is profound. The act of trying to remember — even imperfectly, even failing — is what builds the memory trace that survives time. Reading a clean AI summary does the opposite: it provides the answer before the attempt, short-circuiting the retrieval process entirely.

Key Research

Roediger and Karpicke's landmark study demonstrated that testing produces better long-term retention than additional study time, even when test performance is initially weaker. This "testing effect" or "retrieval practice effect" is one of the most robust findings in cognitive psychology.

Roediger, H. L., & Karpicke, J. D. (2006). Test-enhanced learning: Taking memory tests improves long-term retention. Psychological Science, 17(3), 249–255.

So Is AI the Enemy of Learning?

No. That is not the argument I am making, and I want to be precise about this.

AI can reduce extraneous cognitive load in ways that genuinely help learning. It can clarify confusing explanations. It can provide worked examples that a student can then attempt to replicate. It can offer feedback on output — provided the student produced that output themselves first. It can make knowledge more accessible to learners who have been systematically excluded from it.

The problem is not AI. The problem is a particular pattern of use — one in which AI does the generating, the constructing, the producing, and the learner merely receives and submits.

The question to ask is not "did I use AI?" but "what cognitive work did I do?" If the AI generated and I received, the learning is likely shallow. If I attempted first, struggled genuinely, and then used AI to check, correct, or extend my thinking, something real may have happened.

There is a version of AI-assisted learning that is deeply consistent with what learning science tells us. And there is a version that is simply sophisticated cheating — not of an exam, but of one's own developing mind.

A Note on the Vietnamese Classroom Specifically

I teach in a context shaped by particular pressures: high-stakes examinations, large class sizes, strong family expectations, and a Confucian educational inheritance that values performance and deference. These conditions do not cause AI misuse — but they do shape it.

In a system where the grade matters more than the understanding, and where asking questions can feel like admitting weakness, AI offers something seductive: a way to produce correct outputs without the vulnerability of genuine struggle. The student who uses AI to complete an assignment is not lazy. They are often navigating a system that rewards the output without valuing the process.

This is worth sitting with. The question of whether AI makes us better learners cannot be separated from the question of what we are asking learners to do, and why.

Keep learning. Keep becoming.
— Vy
