The Spacing Effect: One Small Habit That Changes Everything

Ebbinghaus mapped the forgetting curve 140 years ago. The fix has been known ever since. Here is why you are probably still not using it — and how to start today.

VP
Vy Phan
EFL Educator · Doctoral Candidate in Education · Vietnam

Photo by Eric Rothermel on Unsplash · Free to use

One concept. One technique. One thing you can do today. This episode: the spacing effect — the most underused, best-evidenced learning strategy available to anyone with a calendar. If you only change one study habit this year, make it this one.

In 1885, a German psychologist named Hermann Ebbinghaus did something unusual: he used himself as a research subject. He memorised hundreds of meaningless syllable sequences — strings like "DAX", "BUP", "REN" — then tested his own recall at intervals ranging from twenty minutes to a month later. He plotted his results on a graph.

What he found became known as the forgetting curve: a steep, predictable decline in retention that begins almost immediately after learning and accelerates within the first 24 hours.

The forgetting curve — retention without review (after Ebbinghaus, 1885)
After 20 min
~58%
After 1 hour
~44%
After 1 day
~33%
After 1 week
~25%
After 1 month
~21%

One month after learning something new, without review, you retain roughly one-fifth of it. The other four-fifths is gone. Not weakened — gone.

But Ebbinghaus also found the solution. He discovered that if he reviewed material at spaced intervals — returning to it after 24 hours, then a few days later, then a week later — the forgetting curve flattened dramatically. Less total time spent studying. Dramatically better retention. This is the spacing effect: one of the most replicated findings in all of cognitive psychology, and one of the least used.

Why Cramming Feels Better Than It Is

The spacing effect is underused not because students are ignorant of it — most have heard of spaced repetition. It is underused because massed practice, cramming, feels more effective than it actually is.

When you study something repeatedly in a single long session, the material comes back easily. It feels fluent. You feel like you know it. Psychologists call this the fluency illusion — the experience of easy recall in the short term that creates a false sense of durable retention. Spaced review, by contrast, feels harder. There is more forgetting between sessions. Retrieval is effortful. It feels like the technique is not working.

Key Research

Kornell and Bjork (2008) demonstrated that students consistently rated massed practice as more effective than spaced practice — even when their actual test results showed the opposite. The immediate fluency of massed study is misread as evidence of learning. The difficulty of spaced retrieval is misread as failure. Both readings are wrong.

Kornell, N., & Bjork, R. A. (2008). Learning concepts and categories: Is spacing the "enemy of induction"? Psychological Science, 19(6), 585–592.

Robert Bjork's concept of desirable difficulties is the key to understanding this. Not all difficulty in learning is productive — struggling with material that is beyond your current level, or that you have never encountered, produces frustration rather than learning. But the difficulty of spaced retrieval — returning to material after some forgetting has occurred, when recall requires real effort — is among the most productive forms of cognitive effort available.

The struggle to remember across a gap is not a sign that spacing isn't working. It is the mechanism by which spacing works.

Cramming works. It works exactly long enough to pass the exam scheduled for tomorrow morning. Then, within days, most of it is gone.

What the Research Shows About Spacing

The spacing effect has been replicated across content types, age groups, languages, and time scales. It applies to vocabulary, mathematical procedures, conceptual understanding, and motor skills. It is not a technique that works for certain learners or certain subjects — it is a feature of how human memory consolidates information over time.

Key Research

Cepeda and colleagues' 2006 meta-analysis of 254 studies on distributed practice found that spacing consistently produced superior long-term retention compared to massed practice. Crucially, the optimal gap depends on the desired retention interval: for an exam one week away, a gap of one to two days is ideal. For material you want to retain for a year, gaps of weeks to months produce the strongest results. The principle holds across all content types studied.

Cepeda, N. J., Pashler, H., Vul, E., Wixted, J. T., & Rohrer, D. (2006). Distributed practice in verbal recall tasks: A review and quantitative synthesis. Psychological Bulletin, 132(3), 354–380.

There is also a compounding effect that most explanations of spaced repetition understate. Each time you successfully retrieve something after a gap, the memory trace does not simply return to its previous level — it is actually reinforced beyond it. The forgetting curve after each spaced review becomes flatter. The same material reviewed four times over two weeks with appropriate spacing will be retained far longer than material reviewed ten times in a single evening.

This means that spacing is not just a more efficient use of study time. It produces a qualitatively different kind of memory — one that is more durable, more flexible, and more accessible under the conditions (stress, time pressure, real use) when you most need it.

A Note from the Vietnamese Classroom

In fifteen years of teaching English in Vietnam, I have watched a generation of students learn to cram brilliantly. The incentive structure of national examinations rewards the ability to memorise vast amounts of material on short notice — and students respond rationally to that incentive.

What it does not produce is the retained, flexible knowledge they need when they reach university, enter their careers, or try to use their English six months after the exam. The vocabulary that appeared on the test — studied intensively the week before — is typically gone by the time they need to use it in a real conversation.

This is not a personal failing. It is a predictable consequence of studying in the way the system rewards. Spacing does not ask students to study harder. It asks them to study differently — and on less total time, with better results. That is a trade most students would accept if they understood the evidence.

The 48-Hour Rule

The simplest spacing system that actually works
The rule Whatever you study today, schedule a review for exactly 48 hours from now. Not tonight. Not tomorrow morning. 48 hours.
How to review Do not reread. Use the Close-the-Book Test (L5M-01): retrieve what you can recall without looking, then check the gaps. The retrieval attempt is the review — not the rereading.
Then what After the 48-hour review, schedule the next one for 5–7 days later. Then 2–3 weeks after that. Each review: retrieve first, then check.
Time per review 10–15 minutes — far less than a full re-study session, and more effective.
Why 48 hours You are reviewing at the point of maximum productive forgetting — enough has faded that retrieval requires real effort, which is exactly when it builds the strongest memory trace.
Your one action today
Take one thing you studied this week. Schedule a review for 48 hours from now.
Open your calendar right now. Find the last thing you studied — a vocabulary list, a concept, a set of notes, a reading. Create a 15-minute event exactly 48 hours from now, labelled "Review: [topic]". When it fires: close everything, retrieve what you can, then check the gaps.
You do not need an app. You do not need a system. You need one calendar event and the discipline to use retrieval — not rereading — when it fires. Start with one item. If it works, scale up. It will work.
Keep learning. Keep becoming.
