Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development — and What AI Does to It

Vygotsky argued that learning lives in the gap between what a child can do alone and what they can do with guidance. AI can enter that gap. But entering it is not the same as opening it.

VP
Vy Phan
EFL Educator · Doctoral Candidate in Education · Vietnam
LV
Lev Semyonovich Vygotsky
1896–1934 · Soviet psychologist and educational theorist
"What the child can do in cooperation today, he can do alone tomorrow." Core idea: all higher psychological functions originate in social interaction — learning is fundamentally relational, and the quality of that relationship determines what can be learned and how durably.

Photo by Annie Spratt on Unsplash · Free to use

Before you read: think of a time when you learned something genuinely difficult — not through a textbook, not through a video, but with another person. Someone who could see exactly where you were stuck and help you through that specific stuck-ness. Not someone who told you the answer, but someone who guided you to within reaching distance of it. That experience — if you've had it — is what Vygotsky spent his short life trying to understand and name. This article is about whether AI can be that person.

Vygotsky died at thirty-seven, having written more in a decade of productive life than most scholars produce in a lifetime. Tuberculosis killed him in 1934, before he could fully develop the theoretical framework he had been building — before he could respond to critics, before he could follow the implications of his ideas into their most challenging territories. Soviet censorship buried his work for nearly two decades after his death. When it finally reached Western educational psychology in the 1960s and 1970s, it arrived incomplete, selectively translated, and sometimes misread.

It is worth beginning here — with the incompleteness — because the concept most associated with Vygotsky's name has suffered particularly from the distance between what he said and what it has been taken to mean. The Zone of Proximal Development is one of the most cited concepts in educational psychology. It is also one of the most frequently misunderstood.

And the misunderstanding matters enormously now, because AI is being deployed as a ZPD tool — as a scaffold for learning — in ways that rest entirely on how the concept is understood.

What the ZPD Actually Says

Vygotsky introduced the Zone of Proximal Development in the context of a specific and practically urgent question: how should we assess children's learning potential? The standard psychometric approach — measuring what a child can do independently — captures current performance but misses developmental trajectory. Two children who score identically on an independent assessment may have very different learning potentials if one, with guidance, can solve problems far beyond their independent level, while the other cannot.

Core Definition

Vygotsky defined the ZPD as "the distance between the actual developmental level as determined by independent problem solving and the level of potential development as determined through problem solving under adult guidance or in collaboration with more capable peers." The ZPD is not a fixed property of a child. It is a relational property — it exists in the interaction between the learner and the more knowledgeable other, and it changes as that relationship develops.

Vygotsky, L. S. (1978). Mind in society: The development of higher psychological processes. Harvard University Press. (M. Cole, V. John-Steiner, S. Scribner, & E. Souberman, Eds. & Trans.)

Several features of this definition deserve careful attention. The ZPD is defined relationally — it exists between a learner and a more capable other, not inside the learner alone. It is dynamic — it changes as the learner develops and as the relationship deepens. And the "more capable other" in Vygotsky's formulation is not simply someone who knows more. They are someone whose understanding of the learner's current state — their specific confusions, their partial understandings, their particular ways of being stuck — allows them to provide guidance that is precisely calibrated to where the learner actually is.

The three zones — what Vygotsky actually described
Zone of actual development
What the learner can do alone
Tasks the learner can complete independently, without any support. This is the level that traditional assessment measures. Vygotsky argued it shows where development has already arrived — not where it is going.
Zone of proximal development
What the learner can do with guidance
Tasks the learner cannot yet complete alone but can complete with appropriate support from a more knowledgeable other. This is the zone where learning — genuine developmental change — actually happens. The quality of the "more knowledgeable other" determines the size and accessibility of this zone.
Beyond current reach
Too distant for current scaffolding
Tasks that remain inaccessible even with guidance — not because the learner is incapable, but because the developmental gap is currently too large to bridge. Good teaching identifies this boundary and respects it, working at the edge rather than beyond it.

The Social Genesis of Higher Functions

To understand why the ZPD is more than a pedagogical technique, we need to understand Vygotsky's broader theoretical framework. His central claim — developed across his major works — is that all higher psychological functions originate in social interaction before becoming internalised as individual capacities.

This is a profound and counterintuitive claim. The common-sense view of development is that the individual mind develops first and then engages with the social world. Vygotsky inverts this: the social comes first. The capacity to reason, to pay sustained attention, to regulate one's own behaviour, to think in words — none of these arise spontaneously from within. They are first practised in interaction with others, and then, gradually, taken inside.

Core Principle

Vygotsky's "general genetic law of cultural development" states: "Any function in the child's cultural development appears twice, or on two planes. First it appears on the social plane, and then on the psychological plane. First it appears between people as an interpsychological category, and then within the child as an intrapsychological category." This principle — that the interpersonal precedes and constitutes the intrapersonal — is the foundation on which the ZPD rests. Learning is not merely assisted by social interaction; it is constituted by it.

Vygotsky, L. S. (1981). The genesis of higher mental functions. In J. V. Wertsch (Ed.), The concept of activity in Soviet psychology (pp. 144–188). M. E. Sharpe.

What follows from this is significant. If higher functions originate in social interaction, then the quality of that interaction is not incidental to learning — it is constitutive of it. The nature of the relationship between learner and guide, the specific texture of how guidance is given and received, the history of trust and understanding that develops between two people working together over time — all of these shape not just how efficiently information is transferred but what kind of thinking becomes possible.

This is what gets lost when the ZPD is reduced to a metaphor for "adaptive scaffolding" — when it is treated as a technical description of how to calibrate task difficulty rather than as a claim about the social nature of mind itself.

Scaffolding: What Wood and Bruner Added — and What They Changed

The concept of "scaffolding" that is now inseparable from discussions of the ZPD was not Vygotsky's. It was introduced by Wood, Bruner, and Ross in a 1976 paper that described tutoring interactions between mothers and young children. Bruner and his colleagues were drawing on Vygotsky's framework, but they shifted the emphasis in ways that have had lasting consequences.

Key Extension

Wood, Bruner, and Ross defined scaffolding as "those elements of the task that are initially beyond the learner's capability, thus allowing him to concentrate upon and complete only those elements that are within his range of competence." Their characterisation of effective scaffolding identified six functions: recruiting interest in the task, reducing degrees of freedom, maintaining direction, marking critical features, controlling frustration, and demonstrating. This is a useful and practical framework — but it subtly individualises what Vygotsky had understood as relational, and functionalises what he had understood as transformative.

Wood, D., Bruner, J. S., & Ross, G. (1976). The role of tutoring in problem solving. Journal of Child Psychology and Psychiatry, 17(2), 89–100.

The scaffolding metaphor is enormously useful, but it carries risks. A scaffold is a structure. It is erected, used, and removed. It is external to the building it supports. This framing positions the support as a mechanism — something that operates on the learner from outside — rather than as a relationship that is constitutive of the learning itself.

Vygotsky's "more knowledgeable other" is not a scaffold in this sense. They are a participant in a shared cognitive activity whose relationship with the learner — their accumulated knowledge of this particular child, their attunement to this particular confusion, their investment in this particular development — is not separable from the support they provide.

What AI Does to the ZPD: Three Scenarios

AI tutoring systems are now widely described as ZPD tools — systems that identify a learner's current level and provide scaffolding calibrated to their zone of proximal development. This claim deserves careful examination. To examine it, I want to distinguish three scenarios that are often conflated.

Scenario What AI does Vygotskian assessment
Adaptive difficulty calibration Adjusts task difficulty based on performance data to keep the learner in a productive challenge zone Technically useful — approximates one aspect of ZPD thinking. But it is calibration without understanding. The AI adjusts to performance patterns, not to the specific learner's zone of conceptual confusion.
Hint and prompt scaffolding Provides graduated hints when a learner is stuck, reducing degrees of freedom without giving the answer directly Closer to Wood et al.'s scaffolding than to Vygotsky's ZPD. Useful for specific skill development. Does not constitute the interpsychological relationship Vygotsky described as the origin of higher functions.
Socratic dialogue simulation Engages learner in back-and-forth questioning designed to guide them toward understanding Most ambitious claim. Approximates the surface structure of Socratic dialogue. Whether it constitutes genuine intersubjective inquiry — with two minds meeting around a shared problem — is a deep and unresolved question.

None of these scenarios is without educational value. The problem is not that AI scaffolding is useless — it clearly is not. The problem is the claim that it operates the ZPD in the Vygotskian sense. That claim requires that the AI is a genuine "more knowledgeable other" — a social entity whose relationship with the learner constitutes, rather than merely supports, the learning process.

AI can scaffold the gap. But Vygotsky's insight was not that the gap needs scaffolding. It was that the gap is where another mind meets yours — and that meeting is the learning itself.

The Intersubjectivity Problem

The concept that most precisely identifies what AI cannot provide in the Vygotskian framework is intersubjectivity — the shared understanding that develops between two minds engaged in a joint activity. Intersubjectivity, for Vygotsky and for those who extended his work, is not simply mutual comprehension. It is the establishment of a shared psychological plane — a space in which two people are genuinely thinking together, each contributing to and being shaped by the other's perspective.

Key Extension

Rommetveit's development of the concept of intersubjectivity in the Vygotskian tradition describes it as a "temporarily shared social reality" — a space that is created in interaction and that makes possible the kind of joint attention and shared meaning-making that underlies genuine collaborative learning. Crucially, intersubjectivity requires that both parties are genuinely present to the interaction — that each is affected by what the other brings. An entity that processes inputs and generates contextually appropriate outputs is not present in this sense, however sophisticated its processing.

Rommetveit, R. (1985). Language acquisition as increasing linguistic structuring of experience and symbolic behavior control. In J. V. Wertsch (Ed.), Culture, communication and cognition: Vygotskian perspectives (pp. 183–204). Cambridge University Press.

Whether AI can achieve genuine intersubjectivity — whether it can be truly present to a learner's confusion in the way another person can be — is not a question that current AI research can definitively answer. What we can say is that current AI systems, however sophisticated, do not have a stake in the learner's development. They do not remember last Tuesday's breakthrough with this particular student. They do not feel the specific frustration of watching a capable child repeatedly stumble at the same conceptual threshold. They do not bring to the interaction a history of relationship that has calibrated their understanding of this learner's particular way of being stuck.

This is not a technological limitation that will be fixed by the next model release. It is a philosophical question about the nature of the relationship that Vygotsky described as constitutive of learning.

The Vietnamese Classroom: Collective ZPDs and Peer Scaffolding

Vietnamese classroom application — the collective ZPD

Vygotsky's framework, developed in the Soviet Union and elaborated primarily in Western contexts, has typically been applied to dyadic interactions: one more knowledgeable other and one learner. But Vygotsky himself emphasised that peers — not just adult experts — can constitute a "more capable other." The relevant condition is not superior knowledge but the ability to work within the learner's ZPD — to see where they are and support them from there.

This has particular resonance in Vietnamese classrooms. The Confucian educational tradition's emphasis on group solidarity and collective learning — which can seem opposed to Vygotskian thinking at first glance — is actually a potentially rich resource for it. A classroom culture in which students genuinely attend to each other's understanding, where the more advanced student sees their role as supporting the development of peers rather than merely outperforming them, is a culture in which multiple ZPDs can operate simultaneously.

I have seen this work. In classes where I have managed to shift the norm from competitive individual performance to collaborative inquiry — where students explain concepts to each other, where they notice and respond to each other's confusions — the learning that results is qualitatively different from anything an AI tutor can produce. Not because the explanations are more technically precise (they often are not), but because they are relational in the Vygotskian sense: two minds genuinely meeting around a shared problem, each changed by the encounter.

The challenge is that this culture requires conditions — psychological safety, reduced face-concern, a reframing of academic success as collective rather than competitive — that Vietnamese educational structures currently make difficult. This is where AI policy and Vygotskian theory intersect: the most important question about AI in Vietnamese classrooms is not whether it can scaffold individual learners, but whether its deployment supports or undermines the conditions in which genuine social learning becomes possible.

Internalisation: What the Scaffolding Is For

There is one further dimension of Vygotsky's framework that is consistently underemphasised in AI-focused discussions: internalisation. The goal of scaffolded support in Vygotsky's model is not that the learner continues to need that support indefinitely. It is that the support is gradually withdrawn as the learner internalises the cognitive functions that the interaction has made available.

A student who learns to write argumentative essays through sustained interaction with a teacher who asks probing questions, demands justification, and models the back-and-forth of intellectual disagreement — that student is internalising a voice. Not just the teacher's specific comments, but the cognitive practice of holding multiple perspectives, anticipating objections, and revising claims in light of evidence. The external dialogue becomes an internal one.

This is precisely what Vygotsky meant when he said "what the child can do in cooperation today, he can do alone tomorrow." The cooperation is not a crutch. It is the developmental mechanism itself.

The question AI poses to this framework is sharp: what internal voice is the student who relies on AI for essay feedback internalising? If the AI generates the next sentence, identifies the logical gap, or rewrites the unclear paragraph — what cognitive practice is the student developing that they will be able to perform alone?

The answer depends entirely on how the AI is used. An AI that models the questioning process — that asks "what evidence would change your mind about this claim?" — can function within the spirit of Vygotsky's framework. An AI that simply provides the improved version of the paragraph is not scaffolding. It is substitution. And substitution, in Vygotsky's terms, produces nothing that can be internalised — because there was no interaction that generated it, no social plane on which a higher function was first practised before being taken inside.

The intersubjective space of collaborative learning — where, Vygotsky argued, higher psychological functions are first practised before being internalised. Photo: Unsplash

Reading Vygotsky in Đồng Nai

Vygotsky never taught in Vietnam. He never encountered a class of forty students preparing for a national examination. He never navigated the specific texture of face-concern and collective obligation that shapes how Vietnamese students and teachers engage with each other. His ideas arrived here through multiple layers of translation and academic mediation, filtered through Western educational psychology's particular preoccupations.

And yet — reading him carefully, in a Vietnamese classroom rather than a Western one — I find that his most fundamental insight has a particular clarity here. The relationship between teacher and student, between peer and peer, between the one who already knows and the one who is coming to know — this relationship is not incidental to learning. It is, in the Vygotskian sense, where learning happens.

Vietnamese educational culture, for all its limitations from a progressive pedagogical perspective, has never lost sight of this. The deep respect for teachers, the investment in educational relationships, the understanding that learning is a matter of guidance and not merely information transfer — these Confucian inheritances are, in a Vygotskian light, not obstacles to overcome but resources to draw on.

What they require is not replacement by AI but enrichment — conditions in which the relational learning they make possible can operate in service of genuine development rather than examination performance. That enrichment is a pedagogical challenge, a policy challenge, and, ultimately, a cultural one. No algorithm will solve it. But an algorithm, used thoughtfully and in its proper place, need not obstruct it either.

Keep learning. Keep becoming.
