Retrieval Practice: Why Testing Yourself Works Better Than Re-reading

Re-reading feels productive. It is not. The act of pulling information out of your memory — before you feel ready — is what actually builds it.

VP
Vy Phan
EFL Educator · Doctoral Candidate in Education · Vietnam

Photo by Green Chameleon on Unsplash · Free to use

One concept. One technique. One thing you can do today. This episode: retrieval practice — the technique that decades of cognitive psychology research consistently identifies as the most powerful study method available. Not the most popular. The most effective. There is a difference.

Here is a question worth sitting with for a moment: when you finish studying something, how do you know you have learned it?

Most people answer: it feels familiar. I can follow it when I read it back. I understand it when the teacher explains it. These are real experiences. But they are experiences of recognition, not of learning. And recognition, as cognitive psychology has been demonstrating for fifty years, is a very poor guide to what you will actually be able to do with the material when you need it — in an exam, in a conversation, in your work, in a year's time.

The technique that actually predicts durable learning is different from recognition. It is not comfortable. It does not feel like studying. It feels like struggling to remember something you are not sure you know. That experience of effort — of reaching into memory before you feel ready — is retrieval practice. And the evidence for its effectiveness is among the most consistent in all of educational psychology.

The Testing Effect: What the Research Shows

The finding that testing enhances memory — not just measures it — goes back to experiments in the early twentieth century. But the modern research programme that established its scope and mechanisms began in earnest with Roediger and Karpicke's landmark 2006 study.

Landmark Study

Roediger and Karpicke (2006) had participants study prose passages, then either re-study them or take a free recall test. One week later, the testing group retained dramatically more than the re-study group — despite performing less well on an immediate test taken the same day. The finding held across content types and was robust enough that the authors described it as among the strongest and most replicable findings in cognitive psychology. This became known as the "testing effect" or "retrieval practice effect."

Roediger, H. L., & Karpicke, J. D. (2006). Test-enhanced learning: Taking memory tests improves long-term retention. Psychological Science, 17(3), 249–255.

The mechanism is well understood. Every time you successfully retrieve a piece of information from memory, the neural pathways that represent that memory are strengthened — not just reactivated, but physically reinforced. The retrieval process is itself a memory-building act. Re-reading, by contrast, reactivates memory without strengthening it in the same way. It tells you the information is accessible now, in this moment, with the text in front of you. It tells you very little about whether you will be able to access it tomorrow, under pressure, or without the text.

What makes this finding genuinely surprising is its asymmetry. Retrieval practice is more effective than re-studying even when the retrieval fails — even when you attempt to remember something and cannot. The attempt itself, followed by checking the correct answer, produces stronger encoding than simply re-reading the correct answer would have.

The effort to remember — even unsuccessful effort — is doing something to your memory that reading cannot.

Why Re-reading Feels More Effective Than It Is

The persistence of re-reading as a study strategy, despite the overwhelming evidence against it, has a cognitive explanation. Re-reading produces processing fluency — the material feels easy to understand, familiar, smoothly processed. This fluency is experienced as evidence of learning. It is not.

Key Research

Karpicke, Butler, and Roediger (2009) surveyed college students about their study strategies. The most commonly reported strategy was re-reading, used by 84% of students. When asked to predict which strategy would produce better test performance — re-reading or retrieval practice — students systematically underestimated the advantage of retrieval practice. Their predictions tracked how easy the strategy felt, not how effective it was. This metacognitive failure — confusing ease of processing with depth of learning — is one of the most consequential errors students make.

Karpicke, J. D., Butler, A. C., & Roediger, H. L. (2009). Metacognitive strategies in student learning: Do students practise retrieval when they study on their own? Memory, 17(4), 471–479.

This is the core problem: the study strategy that feels most productive is among the least effective, and the strategy that feels most uncomfortable is among the most effective. Students who study by intuition — by doing what feels like learning — will systematically choose the wrong approach.

The Metacognitive Bonus: Retrieval Tells You What You Don't Know

There is a second benefit of retrieval practice that goes beyond memory building: it is the only study strategy that reliably reveals the gaps in your knowledge.

When you re-read, everything looks familiar. There is no signal that separates what you understand deeply from what you merely recognise. When you attempt retrieval — when you close the book and try to produce what you know — the gaps announce themselves immediately and specifically. You find out not that you have "not studied enough," but that you cannot retrieve the third step in a process you thought you understood, or that a specific vocabulary item you read three times is not accessible when the text is gone.

This specificity is educationally valuable. It means that retrieval practice is not just a more effective way to study what you already know — it is a diagnostic tool that generates the precise information you need to study more efficiently.

Key Research

Kornell and Son (2009) showed that students who used retrieval practice were better calibrated — more accurate in predicting what they would and would not be able to remember on a later test — compared to students who re-studied. Retrieval practice does not just strengthen memory; it improves the accuracy of your judgment about what you know. In a Vygotskian frame: it makes your zone of proximal development visible to you.

Kornell, N., & Son, L. K. (2009). Learners' choices and beliefs about self-testing. Memory, 17(5), 493–501.

A Note from Fifteen Years of Teaching

I watch this play out in my EFL classroom constantly. Students who have studied vocabulary lists for three days, who have read and highlighted and reviewed — and who then cannot produce a single target word in a speaking activity two weeks later. The words were recognised. They were never retrieved. They were never truly learned.

The students are not lazy. They studied exactly as they were taught to study. Nobody told them that the act of reading the word again, however many times, is not the same as the act of producing it from nothing when someone asks them a question in English.

The fix is not more study time. It is a different kind of study time — one that feels harder, produces more errors in the short term, and builds far more durable competence in the long term. This is the paradox at the heart of retrieval practice, and it is the reason most students avoid it until someone explains the mechanism clearly enough that the discomfort becomes worth it.

The Blank Page Method

Blank Page Method — retrieval practice in its simplest form
Step 1 Study your material as you normally would — read, listen, take notes. Do not skip this part.
Step 2 Close everything. All of it — book, notes, screen. Completely out of sight.
Step 3 On a blank page or screen, write down everything you can remember about what you just studied. Not what you understood while reading — what you can now produce without looking. Use whatever form helps: sentences, diagrams, bullet points, maps.
Step 4 Check. Compare what you produced against the original. Note specifically what was missing, incomplete, or wrong.
Step 5 Study only the gaps — not the full material. Then repeat Step 2–4 on just those gaps.
Time needed 10–20 minutes. Less if the material is short. More efficient than an hour of re-reading.
Why it works Every retrieval attempt strengthens memory, whether it succeeds or fails. The blank page shows you exactly what to study next — no guessing, no wasted re-reading of things you already know.
Your one action today
After your next study session — whatever it is — close everything and write down what you remember on a blank page.
Not after you finish this article. After your next genuine study session — a class, a reading, a practice set. Close it completely. Take a blank page. Write down what you can retrieve. Then check the gaps. That is your real study list.
The first time you do this, the gap between what you thought you knew and what you can actually retrieve will be uncomfortable. That gap is not evidence of failure — it is the most accurate picture of your learning you have ever had. Most students never get it because they never stop looking at the text.
Keep learning. Keep becoming.
