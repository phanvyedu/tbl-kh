John Dewey and the Classroom That Never Arrived in Vietnam

Dewey argued that learning is experience — that education lives in the encounter between a mind and a real problem. A century later, his most radical idea is still waiting at the door.

VP
Vy Phan
EFL Educator · Doctoral Candidate in Education · Vietnam
JD
John Dewey
1859–1952 · American philosopher and educational reformer
"Education is not preparation for life; education is life itself." Core idea: genuine learning requires active engagement with authentic problems in a democratic community — not passive reception of transmitted knowledge.

Photo by Kari Shea on Unsplash · Free to use

Before you read: think about the most significant thing you have ever learned — not the most important fact, but the learning that actually changed how you see the world. How did it happen? Was it in a classroom? Was it through a textbook? Or was it through an encounter with a real problem, a real consequence, a real other person? Dewey spent his life arguing that the answer to that question should change everything about how we teach. It mostly hasn't. This article asks why — and what that means for Vietnamese education today.

There is a particular quality of silence that I have come to recognise after fifteen years in Vietnamese classrooms. It is not the silence of incomprehension — though it can look identical. It is the silence of a student who has learned, very precisely, that their role is to receive and reproduce rather than to encounter and respond. They are waiting to be told what to know. They have been trained, over years of schooling, to hold the world at a distance — to process it through the textbook rather than to meet it directly.

John Dewey would have recognised this silence. He spent the first half of the twentieth century diagnosing it, fighting it, and building an alternative that was — and remains — genuinely radical. Not radical in a political sense, though it has political implications. Radical in the original sense: going to the root of what education is, and arguing that the root is wrong.

His diagnosis was this: Western schooling had separated education from life, turning it into a preparation for a future that never quite arrives, delivered through a transmission of knowledge that the student has had no part in generating and no genuine reason to care about. The result was not learning. It was, at best, a sophisticated simulation of learning — one that produces students who can pass examinations and cannot think.

A century later, reading Dewey in Đồng Nai, I find that his diagnosis has lost none of its accuracy.

The Core Idea: Experience as the Medium of Learning

Dewey's central argument — developed across a lifetime of writing, most accessibly in Experience and Education (1938) — is that genuine learning occurs through experience, and that not all experiences educate equally. An experience educates when it meets two conditions: it is continuous (it builds on previous experience and opens toward future experience) and it is interactive (it involves genuine engagement between the learner and their environment, including other people).

Core Text

In Experience and Education, Dewey distinguishes between "traditional" education — which imposes adult standards, subject matter, and methods upon those who are only growing toward maturity — and progressive education that takes as its point of departure the experience and capacities of the growing learner. His key criterion for an educative experience: it must not simply be enjoyable or immediately satisfying; it must arrest and inhibit the impulse to act and provoke thought — it must create a problem.

Dewey, J. (1938). Experience and education. Kappa Delta Pi. (Collier Books edition, 1963)

The concept of "problem" is central to Dewey and worth dwelling on. For Dewey, a genuine problem is not a textbook exercise with a known answer. It is a situation of genuine doubt — a moment where existing knowledge is insufficient, where the outcome is uncertain, where the learner has a stake in what happens next. This stake is not manufactured by the teacher through grades and tests. It is intrinsic to the situation itself.

When a student in a science class is asked to predict what will happen when two chemicals are combined — and genuinely does not know, and will find out — that is a Deweyan moment. When a student is asked to memorise what happened when those chemicals were combined, as recorded in a textbook, and reproduce that information in an examination — that is the opposite of a Deweyan moment, however efficiently it transfers the information.

The distinction is not simply about activity versus passivity. It is about whether the learner is a subject or an object of the educational process — whether their mind is meeting the world or being told about it.

Democracy and Education: The Political Dimension

Dewey's educational philosophy cannot be separated from his political philosophy. His most systematic work, Democracy and Education (1916), argues that democratic society requires a certain kind of citizen — one capable of participating in collective inquiry, of revising their beliefs in light of evidence, of engaging with genuine disagreement without retreating into dogma.

This citizen cannot be produced by an education that trains obedience, rewards reproduction, and treats knowledge as fixed and transmissible rather than evolving and constructed.

Core Text

Dewey argued that the school is not merely a preparation for democratic society — it is a form of democratic society, or should be. The classroom community — in which students work together on genuine problems, share their perspectives, revise their ideas, and develop the habits of cooperative inquiry — is not a simulation of democracy. It is democracy in miniature. A school that operates on authoritarian principles cannot produce democratic citizens, regardless of what it teaches about democracy in the curriculum.

Dewey, J. (1916). Democracy and education: An introduction to the philosophy of education. Macmillan.

This is where Dewey becomes genuinely challenging in the Vietnamese context — and where I want to proceed carefully, because the challenge is real but the response requires nuance.

Vietnamese education is not authoritarian in a simple sense. It is shaped by a Confucian inheritance that values learning deeply, that has produced extraordinary intellectual achievements, and that holds teachers in genuine and deserved respect. But it is also a system in which the transmission model of education — teacher knows, student receives — is deeply embedded, and in which the kind of collaborative inquiry Dewey describes is structurally difficult.

Why Dewey's Classroom Never Quite Arrived

Dewey's ideas reached American universities and progressive schools in the early twentieth century, and from there spread — in varying forms and with varying fidelity — across much of the Western educational world. Vietnam's encounter with Western educational ideas came through a different route: first through French colonial education, then through Soviet-influenced models after 1954, then through the pressures of rapid economic development and international integration from the 1990s onward.

Each of these encounters produced its own inheritance. The French colonial model was explicitly transmission-based and hierarchical. The Soviet influence emphasised collective discipline and the systematic development of technical competence. The contemporary pressure of internationalisation has brought curriculum reforms that invoke constructivism and active learning — but often as surface features grafted onto an unchanged deep structure.

This table describes structural conditions, not personal failures. Vietnamese teachers are not choosing the transmission model over Dewey's alternative out of laziness or indifference. They are navigating a system — with its examinations, its class sizes, its accountability structures, its cultural norms — that makes Deweyan practice genuinely difficult and sometimes professionally risky.

The problem is not that Vietnamese teachers don't know Dewey. Many do. The problem is that the system in which they teach was built on the opposite assumptions — and knowing Dewey does not, by itself, change the system.

What Dewey Got Wrong — and Why It Matters

Intellectual honesty requires acknowledging that Dewey was not simply right and Vietnamese education simply wrong. His framework has genuine limitations — and some of them are particularly visible from a Vietnamese classroom perspective.

Dewey's model of the child as an active inquirer, driven by genuine curiosity and intrinsic motivation, reflects particular assumptions about individual development that are more culturally specific than he acknowledged. He was deeply influenced by American pragmatism and by the progressive tradition's faith in the individual as the primary unit of educational concern. The Confucian emphasis on relational learning — on the formation of a person within a network of obligations and connections — captures something real about human development that Dewey's framework underweights.

Critical Perspective

Scholars in comparative education have noted that Dewey's model of democratic inquiry assumes a particular kind of self — one that is relatively autonomous, comfortable with uncertainty, and willing to revise beliefs publicly. These are not universal developmental outcomes; they are products of specific cultural conditions. The criticism is not that collaborative inquiry is wrong, but that Dewey's account of why it is right depends on premises that are culturally situated and not universally shared.

Biesta, G. J. J. (2006). Beyond learning: Democratic education for a human future. Paradigm Publishers.

There is also a more pragmatic critique. Dewey's model requires resources — time, small class sizes, teachers with the pedagogical knowledge and psychological security to facilitate genuine inquiry rather than deliver prepared content. In a system with nearly 120,000 unfilled teaching positions and classrooms of forty or fifty students, the Deweyan ideal is not merely culturally challenging. It is structurally out of reach for most Vietnamese teachers, most of the time.

This is not an argument against Dewey. It is an argument for honesty about the conditions his ideas require — and for thinking carefully about which of his principles can be meaningfully applied within real constraints, rather than treating his full vision as an all-or-nothing proposition.

The Vietnamese Classroom Application: Three Deweyan Moves

Vietnamese classroom application — three moves toward Deweyan practice

Move 1: The genuine question. Dewey's most accessible insight is that learning begins with a genuine question — one to which neither the teacher nor the student knows the answer in advance. In an EFL classroom, this is more achievable than it might seem. Instead of asking "what is the passive voice?" — a question with a predetermined answer — a teacher might ask "why do news articles prefer passive constructions when describing corporate wrongdoing?" This is a real question. It has an interesting answer. And the process of finding it involves using the language rather than analysing it. The question does not require abandoning the curriculum. It requires reframing curriculum content as genuinely interesting problems rather than facts to be transmitted.

Move 2: Consequential output. Dewey argued that experience educates when it has consequences — when what you do affects something beyond the classroom. In Vietnamese schools, this is challenging but not impossible. A student who writes a letter to a real institution, prepares a real presentation for a real audience, designs a solution to a problem that their community actually faces — that student is in Deweyan territory. The consequences need not be dramatic. They need to be genuine. The distance between a graded essay that will be read only by the teacher and a piece of writing that will be read by an actual reader is, educationally, enormous.

Move 3: Narrated experience. Even within a transmission-dominated system, teachers can create space for the kind of reflective experience Dewey valued by asking students to narrate their own learning — not to report what they know, but to describe how they came to know it, what surprised them, what they still don't understand. This seemingly small shift — from "what did you learn?" to "how did learning happen for you?" — enacts Dewey's insight that the process of inquiry is itself the educational content. It also, incidentally, develops the metacognitive awareness that learning science identifies as one of the strongest predictors of academic success.

These three moves do not require a revolution in curriculum or classroom structure. They require a shift in the questions teachers ask and the purposes those questions serve. That shift is — in Dewey's own terms — a beginning, not an end. But beginnings matter.

Dewey in the Age of AI

It would be remiss, in a series published alongside the LAI series on AI in education, to leave Dewey without asking what he would make of the current moment.

I think he would be deeply concerned — not about AI itself, but about the particular ways it is being used. An AI system that generates answers to students' questions, removes the friction of not-knowing, and produces polished outputs that bypass the productive struggle of genuine inquiry — this is the transmission model made infinitely more efficient. It is Dewey's nightmare with better technology.

But he would also, I think, see in AI a genuine opportunity — one that most AI-in-education discourse misses. If AI can handle the transmission functions of education — the delivery of information, the drilling of skills, the correction of routine errors — then what remains for human teachers and human classrooms? What remains is precisely what AI cannot do: the creation of genuine problems, the facilitation of real collaborative inquiry, the cultivation of the democratic habits of mind that Dewey spent his life arguing education should serve.

If AI takes over the transmission, we are finally free to do what Dewey always said education should be for. The question is whether we will recognise that freedom — or simply use AI to transmit more efficiently than ever before.

Dewey's classroom never fully arrived in Vietnam. It never fully arrived anywhere, including in America, where its ideas were born. But it remains, a century later, one of the most powerful articulations of what education could be — and one of the sharpest diagnoses of what it usually is instead.

For a Vietnamese teacher sitting with fifteen years of classrooms behind her and an uncertain pedagogical future ahead, Dewey is not a comfort. He is a provocation. And provocations, as he himself would have insisted, are where learning begins.

Keep learning. Keep becoming.
— Vy
